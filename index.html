Communications Decency Act

Introduction

  • By mid 90's internet was being used more and more
  • People started using it in ways you would expect (anonymity)
  • Society and lawmakers reacted to this the way they always have
    • MPAA, Comic Book Code, etc

CDA

  • Where Communications Act of 1996 comes in
  • Recognizes this fact
  • Encourages creation of filtering technologies
  • Helps parents and others to get these filtering methods
  • Makes ISPs responsible to make these methods available to customers
  • however, some parts raised questions with ACLU (American Civil Liberties Union)

Issues

  • Definition of obscenity very vague
  • Made it a crime to knowingly expose minors to obscene material
  • equired people to provide designated form of proof of age (Credit Cards, id etc.)
    • includes content that is free as well as paid for

Judge decision

  • The last parts of this law violate not only the First Amendment but, also the vague definition violates the 5th and could lead to self incrimination.
  • In the past proof of age like this only applied to commercial transactions.

AOL vs. Kenneth Zeran

Kenneth Zeran sued AOL for defamation of a third party and negligence in removing defamatory statements by one of their users.

After the Oklahoma City Bombings a post by an anonymous user was made on AOL's website about a t-shirt company that would make shirts with offensive slogans about the bombings. The user then posted Zerans phone number and wrote "ask for Ken" on the posting. Zeran was faced with multiple calls by readers of the post when he then called AOL and asked the post be removed. The post was removed, but then the anonymous user posted a similar post with even more offensive t-shirt slogans. Zeran was then repeatedly called and threatened on his life. He alerted AOL again and the FBI.

A radio station was alerted of the post and told its callers to barrage Zeran with phone calls. It was reported that he was getting phone calls every two minutes. AOL refused to post a statement retracting or even acknowledging the post. Zeran then decided to sue AOL and the radio station.

The New York Times reported that AOL won the court case and could not be sued based on the fact that they are not responsible for third party information placed onto their website. The following US code protects AOL: http://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/47/230.

The battle with the radio station was also a loss for Zeran. The Times also reported that since Zeran had no proof that he was harmed by the reporting of the post on AOL's website that the radio station did not commit false light invasion of privacy.

According to the United States statute 230, online companies have a lot of leeway when determing information posted on their sites. The information posted by the subscribers and users is not controlled. The site can however delete the information. In AOL's case against Zeran, they refused to keep tabs on a post where they had multiple complaints. It is unfortunate for Zeran that the site got away with being negligent in the governing of their own website. I think that when multiple complaints are made, a site should be responsible to monitor the post.
